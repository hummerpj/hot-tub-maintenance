# Balancing a 400-gallon hot tub with magnesium chloride and borates

**The combination of magnesium chloride flakes, boric acid buffering, and the dichlor-then-bleach sanitizer method creates a low-maintenance, skin-friendly hot tub with remarkably stable pH.** This approach, refined by the TroubleFreePool community over more than a decade, works because boric acid's buffer system (pKa ~9.2) specifically counteracts the upward pH drift caused by jet aeration — the single biggest chemistry headache in hot tubs. Adding magnesium chloride delivers the "silky water" feel many owners chase, though it's a therapeutic addition rather than a standard water-chemistry parameter. The key constraint governing the entire system is **total dissolved solids (TDS): stay below 1,500 ppm** above your fill water's baseline, or risk corroding heater elements, pump seals, and metal fittings.

---

## Step-by-step fresh fill procedure: the correct order matters

Before filling, **purge the plumbing with AhhSome or a similar biofilm cleaner** — even brand-new tubs harbor biofilm from factory wet-testing. Run jets for 20+ minutes, drain completely, and wipe the shell. Fill through the filter compartment (not the main shell) to prevent air locks. If you have well water or hard municipal water, use a hose pre-filter to reduce metals and minerals.

Once the tub is full and heated, follow this sequence, waiting 15–30 minutes with jets running between each addition:

**1. Sanitize immediately.** Add dichlor granules to reach **5 ppm free chlorine (FC)**. Hot tubs are, as the TFP community puts it, "people soup" — sanitation is always step one. For 400 gallons, this takes about **13.5 grams (2.5 teaspoons)** of dichlor. Each 1 ppm of FC from dichlor also adds ~0.9 ppm of cyanuric acid (CYA), which is intentional.

**2. Test and adjust pH.** Fill water is often 7.8–8.2+. Lower it to **7.2–7.4** using muriatic acid or dry acid (sodium bisulfate). In a 400-gallon tub, roughly **1–2 teaspoons of dry acid** will drop pH by about 0.2 units. Start small, retest after 15 minutes, and repeat.

**3. Lower total alkalinity to 50 ppm** using the "pH/TA dance." Most fill water comes in at 120–200+ ppm TA, but the target for a borate-buffered hot tub is just **50 ppm**. The method: add acid to drop pH to 7.0–7.2, then run jets to aerate. The aeration outgasses CO₂, which raises pH while simultaneously lowering TA. Repeat over several days. This is the most tedious step but the most important — **low TA is the single biggest factor in controlling pH drift**.

**4. Adjust calcium hardness to 120–150 ppm.** Test your fill water's CH. If it's below target, add calcium chloride. For 400 gallons, about **0.8 oz (23 grams) raises CH by 10 ppm**. Pre-dissolve in a bucket first (the reaction is exothermic). This level prevents foaming while staying low enough to avoid scaling if pH spikes.

**5. Continue dichlor until CYA reaches 30 ppm.** Keep dosing dichlor daily for sanitation over the first 1–2 weeks. Track your cumulative CYA. Once it hits **30 ppm**, switch permanently to liquid chlorine (bleach). This is the "dichlor-then-bleach" transition — the foundation of the TFP method.

**6. Add boric acid to reach 50 ppm borates.** Wait until pH and TA are stable before this step. For 400 gallons, add approximately **15 ounces (430 grams, roughly 1¾ cups)** of granular boric acid. Pre-dissolve in a bucket of warm tub water and pour in with jets running. Boric acid has a pH of 3.8–4.8, but at this dose it only drops tub pH by about **0.2 units** — typically no correction needed. This is why boric acid is strongly preferred over borax, which rockets pH to 9+ and requires substantial acid correction.

**7. Add magnesium chloride flakes last.** With all other chemistry stable, dissolve **2–3 cups (460–690 grams)** of food-grade MgCl₂ hexahydrate flakes in warm water and add to the tub. This delivers roughly **200–300 ppm MgCl₂**, leaving comfortable headroom under the 1,500 ppm TDS ceiling. Always use **food-grade flakes** — agricultural or industrial grades often contain up to 5% iron impurities that turn water yellow or green when oxidized by chlorine.

| Parameter | Target range | Why this range |
|---|---|---|
| pH | 7.2–7.8 | Any pH "in the sevens" is acceptable; borates keep it from climbing above ~7.8 |
| Total Alkalinity | 50 ppm | Low TA + borates = minimal pH drift from aeration |
| Calcium Hardness | 120–150 ppm | Prevents foaming; low enough to avoid scaling |
| CYA | 30 ppm | Buffers chlorine without reducing efficacy |
| Free Chlorine | 3–5 ppm (standby) | Based on FC/CYA ratio; shock to 12 ppm after heavy use |
| Borates | 50 ppm (as boron) | Maximum pH-rise buffering; EPA/NSF limit |
| MgCl₂ | 200–300 ppm | Therapeutic feel without excessive TDS |

## Why borates transform hot tub chemistry

Boric acid dissolved in water creates a borate buffer system that specifically **resists upward pH movement**. At 50 ppm, this buffer provides as much resistance to pH rise as 221 ppm of TA provides against pH drop — a finding documented by Richard Falk (ChemGeek), a PhD chemist and TroubleFreePool's most cited chemistry authority. The mechanism is straightforward: at typical spa pH (7.2–7.8), over 93% of the borate exists as undissociated boric acid, which absorbs hydroxide ions generated when jet aeration strips dissolved CO₂ from the water.

The practical result is dramatic. Without borates, hot tub owners typically adjust pH after every session. With 50 ppm borates and TA held at 50 ppm, TFP members consistently report **pH staying stable for a week or more** between adjustments. Borates also deliver several secondary benefits: water feels noticeably silkier, eye irritation drops (boric acid is literally an ingredient in eye drops), and the algaestatic properties reduce organic demand on your sanitizer. Claims of "50% chlorine savings" from borate manufacturers are overstated — the real chlorine benefit is indirect, coming from suppressed algae growth rather than chemical protection of the chlorine molecule itself.

Borates do not degrade, evaporate, or get consumed by chemical reactions. They leave the tub only through splash-out and water replacement, so **replenishment is minimal** — test monthly and top up only if levels drop below 30 ppm.

## Magnesium chloride delivers soft water but demands TDS awareness

Unlike borates, which have established pool-chemistry targets, **there is no industry-standard "ideal ppm" for magnesium in hot tubs.** MgCl₂ is a therapeutic additive. The commercial benchmark is Zodiac's MagnaSpa (a 50% MgCl₂ / 50% KCl blend designed specifically for spas), which doses to just under the 1,500 ppm total salt ceiling. For DIY use with pure flakes, the conservative sweet spot is **200–300 ppm MgCl₂** — enough for noticeably softer, more "silky" water without consuming too much TDS headroom.

MgCl₂ is essentially **pH neutral** in the water (pure MgCl₂ solution sits at ~6.5–7.0 pH) and has no direct effect on total alkalinity since it contains no carbonate or bicarbonate ions. Its primary chemistry impact is on TDS: every gram dissolved adds directly to the dissolved solids load. At 300 ppm MgCl₂ combined with 50 ppm borates, 30 ppm CYA, 150 ppm calcium, and ongoing chloride from bleach additions, you can easily reach **600–800+ ppm above baseline** — consuming half or more of your TDS budget from day one. This typically shortens drain-and-refill intervals by **25–30%** compared to a non-mineral setup.

Two practical cautions deserve emphasis. First, elevated magnesium **interferes with standard calcium hardness titration tests** by overwhelming the Taylor R-0010 buffer that's supposed to precipitate magnesium. You may get falsely high CH readings; the Taylor K-2006/TF-100 kits handle this better than dip strips. Second, magnesium precipitates as Mg(OH)₂ when pH exceeds roughly **10.1** — not a concern in bulk tub water, but potentially an issue inside a salt-water chlorine generator cell where localized pH can spike. This creates a soft white scale on cell plates.

## Dichlor-then-bleach wins the sanitizer comparison

For a system built around borates and magnesium chloride, the **dichlor-then-bleach method is the clear best choice** — lowest cost, best water quality, longest water life, and full compatibility with both additives.

**Dichlor alone** fails long-term because CYA accumulates relentlessly. In a 400-gallon tub, every teaspoon of dichlor adds ~1.5 ppm CYA. Within 3–4 weeks of daily use, CYA can blow past 80 ppm, rendering chlorine increasingly ineffective and forcing a drain. The dichlor-then-bleach method solves this by using dichlor only to build CYA to 30 ppm, then switching permanently to plain liquid chlorine (6–8.25% unscented household bleach or 10–12.5% pool-grade sodium hypochlorite), which adds zero CYA.

**Salt-water chlorine generators (SWCGs)** like the Saltron Mini offer the most hands-off daily experience — set the timer and let the cell produce chlorine. They're compatible with borates and work well in concept. However, combining an SWCG with significant MgCl₂ additions introduces risk: magnesium can **scale the electrolytic cell plates** where localized pH exceeds 10. Standard SWCGs are designed for NaCl, not MgCl₂. If you want both, use NaCl for the generator and add only a small supplemental dose of MgCl₂ for water feel, keeping total salt within the unit's specifications. You'll also still need CYA at 20–30 ppm and occasional acid additions.

**Bromine** is compatible with both additives and excels in warm water (bromamines retain sanitizing power, unlike chloramines), but it costs 30%+ more, accumulates DMH (dimethylhydantoin) from tablets similarly to how dichlor accumulates CYA, and lacks the community-tested optimization ecosystem of the dichlor-then-bleach approach. It's a legitimate choice for people with chlorine sensitivity, but it's not the lowest-maintenance option.

**Post-soak chlorine dosing rule of thumb** (from TFP's ChemGeek): add **~5 fl oz of 6% bleach per person-hour** at 104°F, or ~3.5 fl oz of 8.25% bleach. For a typical 30-minute soak with two people: roughly **2.5–5 fl oz of 6% bleach** afterward.

## Ongoing maintenance becomes genuinely simple

Once your tub is balanced with low TA, borates, and magnesium, the daily routine shrinks to about 60 seconds: test FC and pH with a drop kit or strip, pour in some bleach, done. Here's the full schedule:

**Daily:** Test free chlorine and pH. Add bleach to maintain **3–5 ppm FC**. After each soak, dose bleach based on bather load. With borates buffering, many owners find pH needs adjustment only every **5–7 days** instead of daily — a transformative reduction in chemical fiddling.

**Weekly:** Shock to ~12 ppm FC (or after heavy use). Test TA. Quick rinse of filter. Leave the cover cracked for 15–20 minutes after shocking to off-gas chloramines.

**Monthly:** Test calcium hardness, CYA, borates, and TDS. Deep-clean or soak the filter in a filter cleaner. Top off borates if below 30 ppm. With MgCl₂ in the water, **TDS monitoring becomes critical** — a $10–15 electronic TDS meter is essential equipment.

**Every 2–4 months:** Drain, purge plumbing with AhhSome, refill, and rebalance completely. The standard formula is *(spa gallons ÷ 3) ÷ average daily bathers = days between drains*. For 400 gallons with two people soaking three times per week, that's about **155 days (~5 months)** without MgCl₂, or roughly **3–4 months** with it due to the higher starting TDS. Monitor your TDS meter — if you exceed 1,500 ppm above baseline, it's time to drain regardless of schedule.

**Test kit recommendation:** The **TF-100 kit** (~$70–85 from TFTestKits.net) is the community gold standard, using Taylor reagents with larger bottles of the high-use reagents. The FAS-DPD chlorine test reads up to 50 ppm, which matters for shock-level dosing. Supplement with **borate test strips** (Taylor or LaMotte, ~$12–15 for 25 strips) for monthly borate checks. No consumer test exists for magnesium specifically — track it mathematically based on what you've added, and rely on your TDS meter for the big picture.

## Conclusion

This system works because its three components solve distinct problems simultaneously. **Borates at 50 ppm** eliminate the constant pH creep that makes conventional hot tub chemistry feel like a second job. **Low TA at 50 ppm** works synergistically with borates to let pH find a natural resting point around 7.5–7.6 instead of constantly climbing toward 8+. **Magnesium chloride at 200–300 ppm** adds the therapeutic, silky water feel without participating in — or disrupting — the core chemistry. And the **dichlor-then-bleach method** keeps CYA locked at 30 ppm, chlorine effective, and chemical costs minimal (a gallon of bleach costs under $4 and lasts weeks).

The one trade-off worth accepting: MgCl₂ compresses your water life by consuming TDS headroom. A tub that could go five months between drains might go three to four. For most owners who value the water feel, that's a worthwhile exchange — especially since the borate investment (about $5 worth of boric acid) survives until you drain, and the daily maintenance drops to a quick bleach pour and occasional pH check.
